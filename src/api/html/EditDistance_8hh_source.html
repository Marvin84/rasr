31 /**
32  * Calculation of Levenshtein alignment.
33  *
34  * The Levenshtein distance of two strings \f$A\f$ and \f$B\f$ is the
35  * minimum number of edit operations required to transform \f$A\f$
36  * into \f$B\f$, where a editing operation is either a deletetion, an
37  * insertion or a substitution. By the Levenshtein alignment we
38  * mean the corresponding optimal sequence of pairs \f$(a_i, b_i)\f$,
39  * where \f$A = a_1 ... a_n\f$, \f$B = b_1 ... b_n\f$, and \f$a_i = \epsilon\f$
40  * represents an insertion, \f$b_i = \epsilon\f$ a deletion, and
41  * otherwise \f$a_i \neq b_i\f$ a substitution.
42  *
43  * Here we generalize this notion from sequences to directed
44  * graphs (or equivalently FSAs). The minimization extends not
45  * only over the set of editing operations, but also over all
46  * strings represented in either graph. This has several
47  * applications in NLP, e.g. word error rate, word graph error
48  * rate, phoneme error rate in the presence of pronunciation
49  * variation.
50  *
51  * In the formalism of FSTs the problem can be stated as follows:
52  * The Levenshtein transducer \f$L\f$ is defined as follows: There
53  * is a single state with self transitions. For each \f$a\f$
54  * there are transitions \f$a:a\f$ (correct) with zero cost,
55  * \f$a:\epsilon\f$ (deletion) with cost 1 and \f$\epsilon:a\f$
56  * (insertion) with cost 1. For each \f$a \neq b \f$ there is
57  * transition with cost 1 (substitution). To determine the
58  * optimal Levenshtein alignment of FSAs \f$A\f$ and \f$B\f$ we
59  * just form the composition \f$A \odot L \odot B\f$ and determine
60  * the best path.
61  */
